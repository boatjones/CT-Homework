CIA World Factbook Analysis

Capstone I: R and Excel Project Analyzing Country Data

Pre-Import Work With Data In Excel

The data sourced came from an extract from the CIA World Factbook of various geographic, demographic, and economic factors of all the countries in the world. It was imported into Excel (actually LibreOffice Calc that has great text import/export features) and reviewed. Since the data was all of the worlds countries, some marginal (for the purpose of these studies) countries could be excluded. The first exclusion was all countries lacking a current account balance. This excluded 114 countries such as Tulalu, Greenland, Mongolia, Afghanistan, North Korea, etc.

The next exclusion was all countries lacking an industrial growth rate percent. This excluded 15 more countries such as Libya, Haiti, Burma, Iraq, Lebanon, etc.

The next exclusion was all countries lacking investment as a percent of GDP. This excluded the 3 countries of Laos, Uzbekistan, and Bosnia Herzegovina.

Next, the columns that were to be part of the study were reviewed for completeness. A quick eyeball and in each column proved these columns to be mostly complete and the only one (Railways km) had nulls where the particular countries were too small and/or undeveloped to have rail infrastructure and zero was easily assumed.

With that done, the completed CSV was saved for work.

Conclusions to Study 1:

The p-value represents the probability of seeing this result if the null hypothesis is true. The p-value here is basically zero. Consequently, since the p-class is below the significance level of 0.05, theres strong evidence against \(Ho\). The R-squared value is 0.27. This indicates that about 27% of the variability in GDP per Capita is explained by Transport Density. The F-statistic is 47.7 which is significant.

Because of these values, there is sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis and the findings support the idea that GDP per capita is related to Transport Density. The correlation coefficient of 0.52 shows a moderate positive linear relationship.

Conclusions to Study 2:

The original \(Ho\) and \(Ha\) conclusions of the study were immediately apparent with the first scatterplot. The null hypothesis cannot be rejected and theres no significant relationship between Internet Penetration Percent and GDP Growth Rates. Often, frontier market countries post very high GDP Growth Rates but these growth rates are off of extremely low absolute GDP numbers and are highly variable even within one country year-to-year due to political risk and external factors. Thus the reason for this lack of relationship is logical.

Therefore, I quickly swapped out GDP Growth Rate for GDP per Capita as a more stable measure. This relationship proves out even more strongly than Transport Density. The correlation between the two parameters is 0.90 with a p-value that is minuscule and a huge F-Statistic and R-squared. Therefore, the hull hypothesis can be soundly rejected as there is a definite relationship between GDP per Capita and Internet Penetration.

My philosophical issue with this study is that Internet Penetration may be less a driver of GDP than a measure of wealth on par with number of bathrooms per house, number of cars in household, median age of cars in household, etc. The GDP formula is:

\(GDP = Government Investment + Personal Consumer Expenditure + (Exports - Imports)\)

On this level, the internet penetration is contained in PCE.

Nevertheless, a countrys GDP exists under another curve called aggregate supply which is measured as

\(AS = Labor(L) + Capital(K) + Total Factors of Productivity (TFP)\)

Provided that the bulk of the internet usage is not to look at cat videos but instead to build knowledge, then this goes to increase TFP - the secret sauce of a countrys economic success - that is actually not measured in GDP but the curve residing (usually) above GDP called Aggregate Supply. Declining Aggregate Supply will always force down GDP and a rising Aggregate Supply will always create more space for GDP to increase. The GDP curve cannot remain long above the Aggregate Supply curve and is thus governed by it.
